# First Look Exercise: Live Scenario
## "The Charity Volunteer Platform"

---

## The Messy Request

**From:** Sarah Chen, Operations Manager at *Local Action*, a community charity  
**Subject:** "We need a volunteer scheduling app"

**The 5-minute pitch:**

> "We have about 50 volunteers who help at our food bank, community centre, and youth programmes. Right now we use WhatsApp groups and a Google Sheet. It's chaos. People don't know when shifts are. We miss volunteers who don't show up. Admin staff spend hours chasing people down. We need an app where volunteers can sign up for shifts, get reminders, and we can see who's coming. It should be simple—like Uber but for volunteering. It should only take a few weeks to build and cost barely anything."

**End of pitch.**

---

## Your Job: Run the Intake Gate

You have **15 minutes** to work through this systematically. Use the framework from the one-pager.

---

## Step 1: Strip the Solution (3 mins)

Sarah said: *"We need an app… like Uber… volunteers sign up… get reminders… admin sees who's coming."*

**This is solution bias.** The requester has already decided on the answer.

### Your Task:
**Ask yourself:** What's the *actual problem* beneath these solutions?

Think about:
- What's actually breaking right now?
- Is it the tool (WhatsApp, Google Sheet)? Or something else?
- What would happen if we just *fixed* the spreadsheet instead of building an app?

**Write down** 2–3 sentences about what you think the real problem is.

---

## Step 2: Ask the Two Foundational Questions (3 mins)

### Question 1: "What do you need to be able to do that you cannot do now?"

If you could ask Sarah one question to expose the *real problem*, what would it be?

*Hint: Notice she said "people don't know when shifts are" and "we miss volunteers." These are symptoms. Dig deeper.*

**Write down** what the real user need might be.

### Question 2: "Who exactly are the users? Not broad groups—personas."

Sarah said "volunteers." But that's not specific enough.

**Think:** Are all volunteers the same? 

- A retired nurse with a smartphone?
- A 16-year-old school leaver?
- A parent juggling kids and work?
- Someone with no digital skills?

**Write down** 2–3 different volunteer *personas* (who they are, what they're trying to do, what barriers they face).

---

## Step 3: Identify the Consequences (2 mins)

### Question: "What breaks if we don't deliver this?"

Work through each consequence:

1. **If the chaos continues:** What happens to the volunteers? To the charity? To the people they serve?
2. **Who pays the price?** Volunteers? Staff? The community?
3. **How big is the consequence?** Is someone going hungry? Does a youth programme close? Or is it just inconvenient admin work?

**Write down** the top 3 consequences.

*Hint: Remember "front line weighting." Volunteers under time pressure matter more than admin inconvenience.*

---

## Step 4: Map the Burden (3 mins)

Every solution shifts work somewhere.

**For Sarah's "Uber-style app," ask:**

- **Cognitive load:** How much thinking does a volunteer have to do to sign up? Is the app intuitive? What if someone's not tech-savvy?
- **Operational cost:** Does it save volunteers time? Or does it add steps (download app, create account, log in)?
- **Integration cost:** Do we have to build this from scratch? Or does something already exist that we can adapt?
- **Maintenance cost:** Who fixes it if it breaks? Sarah's a charity—they don't have an IT team.

**Write down:** Where does the burden land? Who's carrying it?

*Red flag: If building a custom app adds burden to small-budget charity staff to maintain it, that's a problem.*

---

## Step 5: Spot the Red Flags (2 mins)

Look back at Sarah's pitch. Circle any phrases that match the **red flag patterns** from the one-pager:

- "It should be simple…"
- "Like Uber…"
- "Should only take a few weeks…"
- "Cost barely anything…"

**What do these red flags tell you?** (Hint: scope underestimation? Unrealistic expectations? Solution bias?)

---

## Step 6: Make the Call (2 mins)

**Using the admission tiers, decide:**

Is this a **Tier 1 (Accept)**, **Tier 2 (Investigate)**, or **Tier 3 (Reject)**?

**Your reasoning should include:**
1. The real problem (not the solution)
2. The consequence (what breaks if we don't solve this)
3. The burden placement (who carries the cost)
4. Red flags you spotted
5. Your admission tier + why

**Write down your decision in 3–4 sentences.**

---

## Step 7: Present Your Decision (1 min)

If you were presenting to a decision panel, how would you frame this?

**Example format:**

> "The real problem is that Sarah's team has no visibility into volunteer availability, which means shifts go unfilled and community services degrade. The consequence is real—youth programmes get cancelled, food bank hours drop. **However,** we spotted three issues: (1) The ask underestimates scope and cost, (2) Sarah's team can't maintain custom software, (3) A process fix might solve 70% of this without code. **Recommendation: Tier 2 (Investigate).** Before we build, we need to explore whether a managed platform (like existing volunteer scheduling SaaS) would work better, or whether the real problem is process, not software."

**Write down** your 3–4 sentence recommendation.

---

## Debrief Questions

After you've made your decision, think about:

1. **Did you fall into the solution trap?** (Did the "app" idea pull you off the scent of the real problem?)
2. **Did you weight front-line burden correctly?** (The volunteers, not the admin staff?)
3. **What's the fastest way to test if this is a real problem?** (Hint: It's not to build an app.)
4. **What would you ask Sarah in a follow-up conversation?**

---

## The Real-World Twist

After the session, Sarah comes back with new information:

> "Actually, the real problem is that our volunteers are mostly 60+, most don't have smartphones, and half live in areas with poor internet. Also, we tried a volunteer app two years ago and nobody used it. We gave up."

**Question:** Does this change your decision? How?

---

## What This Exercise Teaches

- **Solution bias is everywhere.** Even well-meaning people arrive with the answer baked in.
- **Your job is to ask better questions**, not to judge the requester.
- **Consequences, personas, and burden placement matter** more than the proposed solution.
- **Red flags are real.** When you hear "simple," "like Uber," and "just a few weeks," your antenna should go up.
- **Sometimes the answer is "not software."** And that's a good answer.

---

## For the Facilitator

**If students struggle, prompt with:**

- "What would happen if we just cleaned up the Google Sheet? Would that solve it?"
- "Who's actually stressed in this scenario—the volunteers or the admin staff?"
- "If you were a 68-year-old volunteer with a flip phone, could you use this Uber-style app?"
- "Why did the last app fail? That matters."

**If students make a quick Tier 1 decision, push back:**

- "So you'd commit engineering time right now? Even after Sarah said a previous volunteer app failed and her users don't have smartphones?"
- "Let's say you build this. Who supports it next year?"
